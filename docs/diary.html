THE NINAGAWA DIARY

In Nara no sujimichi 奈良の筋道 (The Way to Nara), a detailed daily account of the Jinshin Survey kept by one member of the party, we learn that the British-educated Machida and two of his fellow bureaucrats were mainly interested in call girls. Maiko (apprentice geisha), geisha, and courtesans dot the pages of The Way to Nara, which contains eighty-one pasted albumen paper photographs. The diary was transcribed and published for the first time in 2005.

Ninagawa Noritane蜷川 式胤 (1835-82), the journal’s author, was a Kyoto native and the only elite member who showed little enthusiasm for call girls, although he curtly remarked on their ages, skills, and appearances at several places in his writing. At the end of volume 2 of the diary, in a section entitled “Things to report back to Tokyo,” Ninagawa grumbled that “the other four” bureaucrats on the trip were “girl crazy” (onnagurui 女狂い) and should not be included in its next installment --even though the senior bureaucrats, and not he, were technically leading the trip. With its thoughtfully captioned photographs of buildings and even a female entertainer, The Way to Nara provides a vital look at the earliest years of what we might call art historical photography in Japan, together with contemporaneous responses to those photographs.
